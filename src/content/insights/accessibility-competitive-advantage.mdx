# Accessible design isn't a constraint — it's a competitive advantage

There is a persistent assumption in the industry that accessibility is a cost centre — a
compliance burden to be managed, not a capability to be built. This assumption is wrong, and
it is costing organisations real money.

## The numbers

There are approximately 16 million disabled people in the UK. Their combined spending power —
the "Purple Pound" — is estimated at £274 billion annually. When a digital product is
inaccessible, it excludes these users entirely, often invisibly. The revenue loss is real;
the attribution is simply difficult.

## The business case beyond revenue

Accessibility improvements benefit more than disabled users. They improve:

- **Search visibility** — semantic HTML, logical structure, and meaningful alt text all
  support how AI systems and search engines parse and rank your content
- **Mobile usability** — high-contrast, well-spaced, keyboard-navigable interfaces perform
  better on smaller screens in variable lighting conditions
- **Operational resilience** — accessible products are easier to test automatically, easier
  to maintain, and less likely to require expensive retrofits under regulatory pressure

## The legal reality

The UK's Equality Act 2010 requires reasonable adjustments for disabled users. The EU
Accessibility Act comes into full effect in 2025. Public sector organisations in the UK are
already subject to mandatory accessibility requirements. Private sector exposure is growing.

The question is not whether to prioritise accessibility. It is whether you address it
proactively and gain competitive advantage, or reactively under legal or reputational pressure.
